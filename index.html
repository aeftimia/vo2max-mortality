    Important caveats

    • Clinical cohort bias. The underlying studies included patients referred for exercise treadmill testing — a sicker-than-average population. We use only the ratios (hazard ratios) between fitness levels, anchored to general-population life tables. Whether this over- or under-estimates the true benefit depends on whether fitness-related mortality risk is multiplicative (proportional to baseline) or additive (a fixed excess). Under an additive model, the clinical cohort's higher baseline would make the HR closer to 1 than in a healthier population, meaning we would underestimate the benefit of fitness. The true answer is unknown; the proportional hazards assumption is standard but imperfect.
    • Correlation vs. causation. Observational HRs do not establish causation. Fitness may partly be a biomarker of underlying health rather than a purely modifiable cause of mortality. Intervention RCTs show real but smaller effects than observational HRs.
    • Independence assumption. Risk factors are applied multiplicatively, assuming independence. Real interactions (e.g., diabetes + CAD) are often synergistic, so combined risk may be overestimated or underestimated.
    • Age-constant HRs. Kokkinos HRs are averaged across a wide age range and applied uniformly here. Fitness benefits may vary by age.
    • VO₂ max measurement error. Field estimates (wearables, Cooper test) carry ±5–15 mL/kg/min uncertainty, which can shift category classification.
    • Population generalizability. The underlying study cohorts were predominantly white, middle-aged clinical patients. Results may differ in other populations.
    • Not medical advice. This tool is for educational purposes only. Consult your physician before making changes to exercise or health decisions.
