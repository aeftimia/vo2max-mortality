Overview

This calculator estimates how a person's annual all-cause mortality probability changes across cardiorespiratory fitness (VO₂ max) levels. The approach has three steps:

  1. Anchor to population life tables. The CDC NVSS 2022 US Life Tables give the average annual probability of death q(age, sex) for the US population.
  2. Apply continuous fitness hazard model. Using FRIEND 2022 VO₂ max percentile norms and Kokkinos et al. (2022) continuous hazard ratio (0.86 per +1 MET), we compute a smooth, monotone fitness hazard multiplier normalized so that the population-average equals 1.0. (For historical reference, the original categorical approach using Mandsager et al. 2018 is retained for display comparison.)
  3. Adjust for the user's risk factors. Comorbidity hazard ratios from large published studies are applied multiplicatively to shift the user's personal estimate.

Primary Studies: FRIEND 2022 & Kokkinos 2022

Study designs and data used here

The continuous model implemented in this calculator uses two complementary sources:

  • FRIEND 2022 Percentile Norms: Kaminsky LA, et al. Updated Reference Standards for Cardiorespiratory Fitness Measured with Cardiopulmonary Exercise Testing: Data from the Fitness Registry and the Importance of Exercise National Database (FRIEND). Mayo Clin Proc. 2022;97(2):285–293. DOI: 10.1016/j.mayocp.2021.08.020. The published tables report VO₂ max percentiles (10th–90th, in 10% steps) stratified by decade (20–29 through 80–89) and sex; these percentile curves are the basis for percentiles-to-VO₂ interpolation used by the tool.
  • Kokkinos 2022 Hazard Ratio: Kokkinos P, et al. Cardiorespiratory Fitness and Mortality Risk Across the Spectra of Age, Race, and Sex. J Am Coll Cardiol. 2022;80(6):598–609. DOI: 10.1016/j.jacc.2022.05.031. This work provides a continuous adjusted hazard ratio of 0.86 per +1 MET (95% CI 0.85–0.87) which is applied continuously across VO₂ values (MET = VO₂ / 3.5).

Note: historical categorical results from Mandsager et al. (2018) were used in earlier versions of this calculator. Those five-category outputs have been removed from the default display in favor of the smooth FRIEND+Kokkinos continuous model. The Mandsager study is preserved in the project history for archival cross-checks only.

Why FRIEND deciles and not the old category bins?

FRIEND supplies high-resolution percentile tables by age and sex (10th–90th percentile in 10% steps). The calculator fits smooth, monotone interpolants to these percentile-age grids and evaluates VO₂↔percentile continuously. Using FRIEND deciles avoids clinical-cohort biases in older categorical schemes and supports a normalized, population-mean-preserving hazard multiplier based on Kokkinos's continuous HR.

Continuous Fitness Model: FRIEND 2022 + Kokkinos 2022

Upgrade from categorical to continuous

As of 2026, the calculator has been upgraded from the discrete 5-bin Mandsager categories to a smooth, continuous model. The new approach replaces the step-function hazard ratios with a continuously differentiable function of VO₂ max, derived from:

  • FRIEND 2022 Percentile Norms: Kaminsky LA, et al. Updated Reference Standards for Cardiorespiratory Fitness Measured with Cardiopulmonary Exercise Testing: Data from the Fitness Registry and the Importance of Exercise National Database (FRIEND). Mayo Clin Proc. 2022;97(2):285–293. DOI: 10.1016/j.mayocp.2021.08.020
  • Kokkinos 2022 Hazard Ratio: Kokkinos P, et al. Cardiorespiratory Fitness and Mortality Risk Across the Spectra of Age, Race, and Sex. J Am Coll Cardiol. 2022;80(6):598–609. DOI: 10.1016/j.jacc.2022.05.031

Fitting and normalization

The model is constructed as follows:

  1. Spline fitting: FRIEND 2022 publishes VO₂ max percentiles (10th through 90th) by age decade (20–29 through 80–89) and sex. Smooth, monotone splines (PCHIP) are fitted in both the age and percentile directions, ensuring:
    • VO₂ max decreases monotonically with increasing age (physiologically correct).
    • At fixed age, VO₂ increases monotonically with percentile rank (by definition).
    • C¹ continuity (smooth derivatives) across all interpolated values.
    • Tail extrapolation: percentiles below 10 are clamped to the 10th-percentile curve; percentiles above 90 are clamped to the 90th-percentile curve.
  2. Continuous hazard ratio: Following Kokkinos et al. 2022, the hazard ratio for mortality is:
    $$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{raw}}(\mathrm{VO}_2) = 0.86^{\mathrm{MET}} \quad \text{where} \quad \mathrm{MET} = \frac{\mathrm{VO}_2}{3.5}$$
    This formula is consistent across age, sex, and racial groups (Kokkinos et al. found no significant interactions). The continuous functional form has been shown to fit observational VO₂ max mortality data more accurately than categorical models.
  3. Population-mean normalization: To ensure that the population-average fitness hazard multiplier equals 1.0 (preserving the baseline life-table mortality), a normalization constant \(k(\text{age}, \text{sex})\) is computed:
    $$k(\text{age}, \text{sex}) = \frac{1}{\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{HR}_{\text{raw}}]} = \frac{1}{\int_0^1 0.86^{\mathrm{VO}_2(p) / 3.5} \, dp}$$
    where the expectation is taken over a uniform percentile rank \(p \in [0, 1]\) and \(\mathrm{VO}_2(p)\) is the spline-interpolated value at percentile \(p\). This integral is computed numerically for each age and sex using Gaussian quadrature.
  4. Normalized hazard: The user's fitness hazard multiplier is:
    $$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{fitness}}(\mathrm{age}, \mathrm{VO}_2, \text{sex}) = k(\text{age}, \text{sex}) \times 0.86^{\mathrm{VO}_2 / 3.5}$$
    This ensures:
    • At the population-average VO₂ (50th percentile), \(\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{HR}_{\text{fitness}}] = 1.0\).
    • The population-weighted mortality remains anchored to the SSA life table.
    • Individual VO₂ values map smoothly and monotonically to hazard multipliers.

Why integrate rather than anchor at the median?

Normalization is chosen so the expected (population-averaged) fitness hazard equals 1.0: \(\mathbb{E}[k\cdot 0.86^{\mathrm{MET}}]=1\). Anchoring at the median (\(k=1/\mathrm{HR}_{\text{raw}}(\mathrm{VO}_{50})\)) would not guarantee this because of the convexity of the exponential: \(\mathbb{E}[0.86^{\mathrm{MET}}]\neq 0.86^{\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{MET}]}\). Integrating over the full percentile distribution preserves the true population mean and avoids bias from median-anchoring.

Sensitivity: hazard ratio range

Kokkinos et al. report the adjusted HR as 0.86 (95% CI: 0.85–0.87) per +1 MET. To propagate uncertainty, the calculator provides plausible ranges using the CI bounds. Note that this is a simplified uncertainty propagation; a full Bayesian posterior would require joint modeling of the spline parameters and the HR CI.

Mathematical Derivation

Step 1 — Population baseline

Let \(q_{\text{pop}}\) = SSA life table q(age, sex). This is the average annual mortality for a person of this age and sex in the US general population.

Step 2 — Fitness-stratified mortality

The Mandsager hazard ratios tell us how much more or less likely each fitness group is to die relative to the others. To convert these relative ratios into absolute mortality rates, we need to find the scale factor that makes the population-weighted average match \(q_{\text{pop}}\).

If mortality is proportional to HR, then each group's mortality is some constant \(c\) times its HR. The population average constrains that constant:

$$q_{\text{pop}} = \sum_i f_i \cdot c \cdot \mathrm{HR}_i = c \cdot \underbrace{\sum_i f_i \cdot \mathrm{HR}_i}_{\bar{H}}$$

Solving for \(c\) and substituting back gives the group-specific mortality directly:

In words: each group's mortality equals the population rate, scaled by how far that group's HR is above or below the population-weighted average HR. No single group plays a privileged role — every category is treated symmetrically.

Step 3 — User risk factor adjustment

The user's personal mortality at each fitness level is:

$$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}} = \prod_k \mathrm{HR}_k \qquad \text{(product of selected comorbidity HRs)}$$ $$q_{\text{user},i} = q_i \times \mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}} \qquad \text{(for each fitness category } i \text{)}$$

Independence assumption: This treats risk factors as multiplying independently. Real comorbidities often interact (e.g., diabetes + CAD is worse than the product predicts). This is a known limitation; see caveats below.

Step 4 — Excess mortality

$$\Delta q(\text{target}) = q_{\text{user}}[\text{target}] - q_{\text{user}}[\text{current}]$$

Negative \(\Delta q\) indicates better fitness (reduced mortality); positive \(\Delta q\) indicates worse fitness.

Interpretation: "Moving from Level A to Level B saves/costs the equivalent of N [activity] per year in additional annual mortality risk." This is a statistical comparison — it does not mean literally performing those activities is equivalent in any other sense.

Step 6 — Plausible range (CI propagation)

We propagate the 95% CIs from the Mandsager HR table conservatively. The same formula applies, but we vary all HRs to their CI bounds simultaneously:

$$\bar{H}_{\text{hi}} = \sum_i f_i \cdot \mathrm{HR}_{i,\text{CI upper}}, \qquad \bar{H}_{\text{lo}} = \sum_i f_i \cdot \mathrm{HR}_{i,\text{CI lower}}$$ $$q_{i,\text{lo}} = q_{\text{pop}} \times \frac{\mathrm{HR}_{i,\text{CI lower}}}{\bar{H}_{\text{hi}}}, \qquad q_{i,\text{hi}} = q_{\text{pop}} \times \frac{\mathrm{HR}_{i,\text{CI upper}}}{\bar{H}_{\text{lo}}}$$

These are labeled "plausible range" rather than "95% CI" because using all HR bounds simultaneously is conservative — the true 95% CI would be narrower.

Step 7 — Life expectancy

For each fitness category, we compute expected remaining years of life starting at the user's current age \(a\), integrating the shifted survival curve through age 119.

Mortality multiplier. The user's mortality in category \(i\) at age \(a\) is \(q_{\text{user},i}(a)\). We define a dimensionless multiplier relative to the life-table baseline:

$$m_i = \frac{q_{\text{user},i}(a)}{q_{\text{pop}}(a)}$$

This multiplier combines the fitness HR and any selected comorbidity HRs: \(m_i = (\mathrm{HR}_i / \bar{H}) \times \mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}}\). We apply it uniformly to every future age in the life table — a simplifying assumption discussed below.

Survival function. Starting at age \(a\), the probability of surviving to age \(x\) under multiplier \(m_i\) is:

$$S_i(x) = \prod_{t=a}^{x-1} \bigl(1 - \min(m_i \cdot q_{\text{SSA}}(t,\text{sex}),\; 1)\bigr)$$

Expected remaining years. Standard actuarial identity: remaining life expectancy equals the area under the survival curve, approximated by the discrete sum

$$\mathrm{LE}_i = \sum_{x=a}^{118} S_i(x+1) = \sum_{x=a}^{118} \prod_{t=a}^{x} \bigl(1 - m_i \cdot q_{\text{SSA}}(t,\text{sex})\bigr)$$

(each term \(S_i(x+1)\) is the probability of being alive at the start of year \(x+1\), and each year of survival contributes exactly 1 to the sum.)

Life expectancy delta. The displayed "years gained or lost" is the difference in remaining LE relative to the user's current fitness category \(c\):

$$\Delta\mathrm{LE}(\text{target}) = \mathrm{LE}_{\text{target}} - \mathrm{LE}_{c}$$

Simplifying assumption and its implications. We apply the multiplier \(m_i\) uniformly across all future ages. In reality, fitness-associated mortality HRs may attenuate at older ages, and a person's fitness category is not fixed for life. This means the LE delta estimates are likely upper bounds on the causal benefit of a permanent fitness change. They should be interpreted as illustrative comparisons, not clinical predictions.

The risk equivalent calculation converts an annual excess mortality fraction Δq into N equivalent single-event risks: N = Δq / (mortality per event). For example, if moving to a lower fitness category increases your annual mortality by 0.05%, and one base jump carries a 0.043% mortality risk, then 0.05% / 0.043% ≈ 1.2 base jumps per year carries an equivalent annual mortality burden.

Limitations

  1. Clinical cohort bias. Mandsager's cohort is referred patients, not a general population sample. We use only the relative hazard ratios, anchored to general-population life tables. Whether this over- or under-estimates the true fitness benefit depends on the underlying model: under proportional hazards (multiplicative risk), the HRs transfer directly; under an additive excess-risk model, the clinical cohort's higher baseline mortality compresses HRs toward 1, meaning we would underestimate fitness benefits in healthier populations. The true relationship is unknown.
  2. Causality. Observational HRs do not prove causation. Low fitness may partly reflect underlying disease (reverse causation) rather than being a modifiable risk factor. Exercise intervention trials show real but smaller mortality benefits than the observational HRs suggest.
  3. Risk factor independence. Multiplying HRs assumes each risk factor's effect is independent. Many conditions interact synergistically, so combined risk estimates may be biased in either direction.
  4. Age-constant HRs. Mandsager HRs are averaged across the full age range of the cohort (mean age 53) and are applied uniformly across all ages in this calculator. Fitness-mortality associations likely vary by age.
  5. VO₂ max measurement error. Field estimates from wearables or exercise tests carry ±5–15 mL/kg/min uncertainty, which is large enough to shift fitness category classification.
  6. Proportionality across the life course. The back-calculation assumes the fitness HRs hold at every age simultaneously. The true relationship may vary across the lifespan.
  7. Population generalizability. The Mandsager cohort was predominantly White, middle-aged, from a tertiary referral center in the US Midwest. Results may differ in other racial, ethnic, age, or geographic groups.
