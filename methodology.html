Not medical advice. This tool is for educational and research purposes only. All estimates carry substantial uncertainty. Consult a physician before making health decisions.

Overview

This calculator estimates how a person's annual all-cause mortality probability changes across cardiorespiratory fitness (VO₂ max) levels. The approach has three steps:

  1. Anchor to population life tables. The CDC NVSS 2021 US Life Tables give the average annual probability of death q(age, sex) for the US population.
  2. Back-calculate fitness-stratified mortality. Using hazard ratios from Mandsager et al. (2018), we derive the mortality rate for each of five fitness categories such that the population-weighted average equals the life table value.
  3. Adjust for the user's risk factors. Comorbidity hazard ratios from large published studies are applied multiplicatively to shift the user's personal estimate.

Excess mortality between fitness levels is then expressed as equivalent annual occurrences of risky activities (base jumps, skydives, CT scans).

Primary Study: Mandsager et al. 2018

Full citation: Mandsager K, Harb S, Cremer P, Phelan D, Nissen SE, Jaber W. Association Between Cardiorespiratory Fitness and Long-term Mortality Among Adults Undergoing Exercise Treadmill Testing. JAMA Netw Open. 2018;1(6):e183605. DOI: 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2018.3605. Open access: PMC6324439.

Study design

Retrospective cohort study of 122,007 patients who underwent exercise treadmill testing at Cleveland Clinic between 1991 and 2014. Median follow-up 8.4 years; 13,637 deaths (1.1 million person-years). Cox proportional hazards models, adjusted for age, sex, year of test, and multiple comorbidities.

Fitness categories

Participants were classified into five groups by age- and sex-specific percentile rank within the cohort:

  • Low: < 25th percentile
  • Below Average: 25th–49th percentile
  • Above Average: 50th–74th percentile
  • High: 75th–97.5th percentile
  • Elite: ≥ 97.5th percentile

VO₂ max category boundaries

Table 1 of the paper reports VO₂ max ranges (originally in METs; converted here via VO₂ = METs × 3.5) by age group and sex. These are the boundaries used in the calculator to classify a user's VO₂ max. Using the paper's own boundaries ensures that the HR applied matches the fitness level that was actually studied.

Key limitation: clinical cohort

Mandsager's patients were referred for exercise treadmill testing — they are not a representative general population sample. Low-fitness patients in a cardiology referral cohort are likely sicker than low-fitness people in the general population, which could inflate the hazard ratio for Low vs Elite fitness. We assume the relative hazard ratios between categories transfer to the general population; this is an assumption, not a proven fact.

Baseline Mortality: SSA Life Table

Source: Social Security Administration. Period Life Table, 2021. ssa.gov/oact/STATS/table4c6.html. Public domain.

The q(x) values — probability of dying within one year at exact age x — come from the CDC National Vital Statistics Reports, Vol. 72, No. 12 (November 2023), specifically Table 2 (males) and Table 3 (females) of "United States Life Tables, 2021." All integer ages 0–99 are transcribed directly from those tables. Ages 100–119 use log-linear extrapolation anchored at age 99, matching standard actuarial practice for open-ended intervals. Source files: ftp.cdc.gov/pub/Health_Statistics/NCHS/Publications/NVSR/72-12/ (public domain — US federal government work).

Mathematical Derivation

Step 1 — Population baseline

Let qpop = SSA life table q(age, sex). This is the average annual mortality for a person of this age and sex in the US general population.

Step 2 — Back-calculation of category-specific mortality

Under a proportional hazards model, mortality in each fitness category is:

$$q_i = \mathrm{HR}_i \times q_{\text{Low}}, \qquad i \in \{\text{Low, BelowAvg, AboveAvg, High, Elite}\}$$

The population-weighted average must equal \(q_{\text{pop}}\):

$$q_{\text{pop}} = \sum_i f_i \cdot q_i = \sum_i f_i \cdot \mathrm{HR}_i \cdot q_{\text{Low}} = q_{\text{Low}} \cdot W$$ $$W = \sum_i f_i \cdot \mathrm{HR}_i = \underbrace{0.2392 \times 1.00}_{\text{Low}} + \underbrace{0.2227 \times 0.71}_{\text{BelowAvg}} + \underbrace{0.2614 \times 0.50}_{\text{AboveAvg}} + \underbrace{0.2474 \times 0.39}_{\text{High}} + \underbrace{0.0293 \times 0.20}_{\text{Elite}} \approx 0.630$$ $$\therefore \quad q_{\text{Low}} = \frac{q_{\text{pop}}}{W}, \qquad q_i = q_{\text{Low}} \cdot \mathrm{HR}_i$$

The fractions f_i are derived from sample sizes reported in Mandsager Table 1: Low (29,181), Below Average (27,172), Above Average (31,897), High (30,187), Elite (3,570), total n = 122,007.

Step 3 — User risk factor adjustment

The user's personal mortality at each fitness level is:

$$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}} = \prod_k \mathrm{HR}_k \qquad \text{(product of selected comorbidity HRs)}$$ $$q_{\text{user},i} = q_i \times \mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}} \qquad \text{(for each fitness category } i \text{)}$$

Independence assumption: This treats risk factors as multiplying independently. Real comorbidities often interact (e.g., diabetes + CAD is worse than the product predicts). This is a known limitation; see caveats below.

Step 4 — Excess mortality

$$\Delta q(\text{target}) = q_{\text{user}}[\text{target}] - q_{\text{user}}[\text{current}]$$

Negative \(\Delta q\) indicates better fitness (reduced mortality); positive \(\Delta q\) indicates worse fitness.

Step 5 — Risk equivalents

Annual excess mortality Δq is expressed as N equivalent events:

$$N_{\text{activity}} = \frac{\Delta q}{p_{\text{activity}}}$$ $$N_{\text{base jumps}} = \frac{\Delta q}{1/2317} = \Delta q \times 2317$$ $$N_{\text{skydives}} = \frac{\Delta q}{10/3{,}700{,}000} = \Delta q \times 370{,}000$$ $$N_{\text{CT scans}} = \frac{\Delta q}{0.0005} = \Delta q \times 2000$$

Interpretation: "Moving from Level A to Level B saves/costs the equivalent of N [activity] per year in additional annual mortality risk." This is a statistical comparison — it does not mean literally performing those activities is equivalent in any other sense.

Step 6 — Plausible range (CI propagation)

We propagate the 95% CIs from the Mandsager HR table conservatively:

$$W_{\text{lo}} = \sum_i f_i \cdot \mathrm{HR}_{i,\text{CI upper}} \implies q_{\text{Low,lo}} = \frac{q_{\text{pop}}}{W_{\text{lo}}}$$ $$W_{\text{hi}} = \sum_i f_i \cdot \mathrm{HR}_{i,\text{CI lower}} \implies q_{\text{Low,hi}} = \frac{q_{\text{pop}}}{W_{\text{hi}}}$$ $$q_{i,\text{lo}} = q_{\text{Low,lo}} \times \mathrm{HR}_{i,\text{CI lower}}, \qquad q_{i,\text{hi}} = q_{\text{Low,hi}} \times \mathrm{HR}_{i,\text{CI upper}}$$

These are labeled "plausible range" rather than "95% CI" because using all HR bounds simultaneously is conservative — the true 95% CI would be narrower.

Step 7 — Life expectancy

For each fitness category, we compute expected remaining years of life starting at the user's current age \(a\), integrating the shifted survival curve through age 119.

Mortality multiplier. The user's mortality in category \(i\) at age \(a\) is \(q_{\text{user},i}(a)\). We define a dimensionless multiplier relative to the life-table baseline:

$$m_i = \frac{q_{\text{user},i}(a)}{q_{\text{pop}}(a)}$$

This multiplier combines the fitness HR and any selected comorbidity HRs: \(m_i = \mathrm{HR}_i / W \times \mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}}\). We apply it uniformly to every future age in the life table — a simplifying assumption discussed below.

Survival function. Starting at age \(a\), the probability of surviving to age \(x\) under multiplier \(m_i\) is:

$$S_i(x) = \prod_{t=a}^{x-1} \bigl(1 - \min(m_i \cdot q_{\text{SSA}}(t,\text{sex}),\; 1)\bigr)$$

Expected remaining years. Standard actuarial identity: remaining life expectancy equals the area under the survival curve, approximated by the discrete sum

$$\mathrm{LE}_i = \sum_{x=a}^{118} S_i(x+1) = \sum_{x=a}^{118} \prod_{t=a}^{x} \bigl(1 - m_i \cdot q_{\text{SSA}}(t,\text{sex})\bigr)$$

(each term \(S_i(x+1)\) is the probability of being alive at the start of year \(x+1\), and each year of survival contributes exactly 1 to the sum.)

Life expectancy delta. The displayed "years gained or lost" is the difference in remaining LE relative to the user's current fitness category \(c\):

$$\Delta\mathrm{LE}(\text{target}) = \mathrm{LE}_{\text{target}} - \mathrm{LE}_{c}$$

Simplifying assumption and its implications. We apply the multiplier \(m_i\) uniformly across all future ages. In reality, fitness-associated mortality HRs may attenuate at older ages, and a person's fitness category is not fixed for life. This means the LE delta estimates are likely upper bounds on the causal benefit of a permanent fitness change. They should be interpreted as illustrative comparisons, not clinical predictions.

The risk equivalent calculation converts an annual excess mortality fraction Δq into N equivalent single-event risks: N = Δq / (mortality per event). For example, if moving to a lower fitness category increases your annual mortality by 0.05%, and one base jump carries a 0.043% mortality risk, then 0.05% / 0.043% ≈ 1.2 base jumps per year carries an equivalent annual mortality burden.

Limitations

  1. Clinical cohort bias. Mandsager's cohort is referred patients, not a general population sample. The absolute mortality rates in low-fitness patients may be higher than in the general population, potentially inflating the apparent benefit of fitness. Using only the HRs (ratios) mitigates but does not eliminate this concern.
  2. Causality. Observational HRs do not prove causation. Low fitness may partly reflect underlying disease (reverse causation) rather than being a modifiable risk factor. Exercise intervention trials show real but smaller mortality benefits than the observational HRs suggest.
  3. Risk factor independence. Multiplying HRs assumes each risk factor's effect is independent. Many conditions interact synergistically, so combined risk estimates may be biased in either direction.
  4. Age-constant HRs. Mandsager HRs are averaged across the full age range of the cohort (mean age 53) and are applied uniformly across all ages in this calculator. Fitness-mortality associations likely vary by age.
  5. VO₂ max measurement error. Field estimates from wearables or exercise tests carry ±5–15 mL/kg/min uncertainty, which is large enough to shift fitness category classification.
  6. Proportionality across the life course. The back-calculation assumes the fitness HRs hold at every age simultaneously. The true relationship may vary across the lifespan.
  7. Population generalizability. The Mandsager cohort was predominantly White, middle-aged, from a tertiary referral center in the US Midwest. Results may differ in other racial, ethnic, age, or geographic groups.
