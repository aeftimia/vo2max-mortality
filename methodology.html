Overview

This calculator estimates how a person's annual all-cause mortality probability changes across cardiorespiratory fitness (VO₂ max) levels. The approach has three steps:

  1. Anchor to population life tables. The ${citeLink('nvss2022', 'CDC NVSS 2022 US Life Tables')} give the average annual probability of death q(age, sex) for the US population.
  2. Apply continuous fitness hazard model. Using ${citeLink('friend2022', 'FRIEND 2022')} VO₂ max percentile norms and ${citeLink('kokkinos2022', 'Kokkinos et al. 2022')} continuous hazard ratio (0.86 per +1 MET), we compute a smooth, monotone fitness hazard multiplier normalized so that the population-average equals 1.0.
  3. Adjust for the user's risk factors. Comorbidity hazard ratios from large published studies are applied multiplicatively to shift the user's personal estimate.

Fitting and normalization

The model is constructed as follows:

  1. Spline fitting: FRIEND 2022 publishes VO₂ max percentiles (10th through 90th) by age decade (20–29 through 80–89) and sex. Two spline methods are used to interpolate this data:
    • Age direction — monotone quadratic histospline: The published values are treated as bin averages over each age decade (not point estimates at midpoints). A C¹ piecewise-quadratic spline is fitted such that the integral over each decade bin exactly reproduces the published value. This is more faithful to how the FRIEND data is reported. Monotonicity (VO₂ decreasing with age) is enforced.
    • Percentile direction — monotone cubic Hermite (PCHIP): At each integer age, the 9 percentile values (10th–90th) from the age histosplines are extended to the 0th and 100th percentile and interpolated with a C¹ monotone piecewise-cubic spline (Fritsch–Carlson algorithm, via scipy's PchipInterpolator) across all 11 knots [0, 10, 20, …, 90, 100]:
      • 0th percentile: Set to ~10 mL/kg/min, the hard physiological floor for ambulatory humans (${citeLink('shephard2009', 'Shephard 2009')}; clinical heart failure literature places the survivable minimum at ~10–12 mL/kg/min).
      • 100th percentile: Extrapolated by mirroring the 80th→90th gap: VO₂(100) = VO₂(90) + [VO₂(90) − VO₂(80)].
    • Both directions guarantee: monotonicity, C¹ continuity, and exact polynomial integration. Gauss-Legendre quadrature is used for the exponential HR integral over each polynomial piece.
  2. Continuous hazard ratio: Following Kokkinos et al. 2022, the hazard ratio for mortality is:
    $$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{raw}} = 0.86^{\,\mathrm{MET}}, \quad \mathrm{MET} = \mathrm{VO}_2 / 3.5$$
    This formula is consistent across age, sex, and racial groups (Kokkinos et al. found no significant interactions). The continuous functional form has been shown to fit observational VO₂ max mortality data more accurately than categorical models.
  3. Population-mean normalization: To ensure that the population-average fitness hazard multiplier equals 1.0 (preserving the baseline life-table mortality), a normalization constant \(k(\text{age}, \text{sex})\) is computed:
    $$k(a,s) = \frac{1}{\int_0^1 0.86^{\,\mathrm{VO}_2(p,a,s)\,/\,3.5} \, dp}$$
    where the expectation is taken over a uniform percentile rank \(p \in [0, 1]\) and \(\mathrm{VO}_2(p)\) is the piecewise spline value at percentile \(p\). The integral is computed using 16-point Gauss-Legendre quadrature on each polynomial piece.

    Because the normalization constant depends on the HR-per-MET value, three separate constants are precomputed for each (age, sex): one for the central estimate (0.86) and one for each bound of the 95% CI (0.85, 0.87). This ensures that plausible-range calculations maintain the population-average HR = 1.0 property at every CI level.
  4. Normalized hazard: The user's fitness hazard multiplier is:
    $$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{fit}}(a, s, \mathrm{VO}_2) = k(a,s) \cdot 0.86^{\,\mathrm{VO}_2 / 3.5}$$
    This ensures:
    • At the population-average VO₂ (50th percentile), \(\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{HR}_{\text{fitness}}] = 1.0\).
    • The population-weighted mortality remains anchored to the SSA life table.
    • Individual VO₂ values map smoothly and monotonically to hazard multipliers.

Sensitivity: hazard ratio range

Kokkinos et al. report the adjusted HR as 0.86 (95% CI: 0.85–0.87) per +1 MET. To propagate uncertainty, the calculator computes plausible ranges using each CI bound with its own normalization constant (so population-average HR = 1.0 is maintained at every CI level). Note that this is a simplified uncertainty propagation; a full Bayesian posterior would require joint modeling of the spline parameters and the HR CI.

Mathematical Model: Continuous Fitness Hazard

Overview

The model uses a continuous, smooth, age/sex-specific fitness hazard function based on:

  • FRIEND 2022 normative VO₂ max percentiles: Age/sex-specific piecewise polynomial splines (monotone quadratic histospline for age, monotone cubic Hermite/PCHIP for percentile) that give VO₂ max at any percentile rank (0–100) for ages 20–89.
  • Kokkinos 2022 hazard ratio: A constant 0.86 per +1 MET (=+3.5 mL/kg/min VO₂), consistent across age, sex, and race.
  • Integral normalization: The population-averaged fitness hazard ratio equals exactly 1.0, ensuring the population baseline mortality from SSA life tables is preserved.

Step 1 — Baseline population mortality

Let \(q_{\text{pop}}(a, s)\) = mortality probability from the ${citeLink('nvss2022')} for age \(a\) and sex \(s\). This is the average annual mortality for the US population.

Step 2 — Fitness percentile to VO₂ max conversion

For a user with VO₂ max \(\text{VO}_2\) mL/kg/min, we estimate their fitness percentile \(p\) (relative rank among healthy US adults of the same age and sex) using the inverse of the fitted ${citeLink('friend2022')} piecewise-cubic (PCHIP) percentile splines. The spline covers the full 0–100 range with physiological bounds at the extremes (${citeLink('shephard2009', 'Shephard 2009')}).

Step 3 — Continuous fitness hazard ratio

The ${citeLink('kokkinos2022')} hazard ratio is \(\mathrm{HR} = 0.86^{\text{MET}}\), where \(\text{MET} = \text{VO}_2 / 3.5\). This gives the relative mortality risk compared to some reference.

To normalize so the population-average hazard equals 1.0, we integrate over the uniform percentile distribution:

$$\bar{H}(a,s) = \int_0^1 0.86^{\,\mathrm{MET}(p,a,s)} \, dp$$

The normalization constant is \(k(a,s) = 1 / \bar{H}(a,s)\). This is pre-computed offline for all ages 20–89 and both sexes.

The user's normalized fitness hazard at VO₂ max \(\text{VO}_2\) is:

$$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{fit}}(a, s, \mathrm{VO}_2) = k(a,s) \cdot 0.86^{\,\mathrm{VO}_2 / 3.5}$$

Why integrate rather than anchor at the median? Because the exponential function is strictly convex, \(E[0.86^{\text{MET}}] \neq 0.86^{E[\text{MET}]}\). Integrating over the full percentile distribution preserves the actual population mean and ensures the population-average mortality hazard equals exactly 1.0. This is more statistically rigorous than anchoring at a single percentile.

Step 4 — User risk factor adjustment

Comorbidity hazard ratios from large cohort studies are applied multiplicatively:

$$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}} = \mathrm{HR}_{\text{fit}} \cdot \prod_i \mathrm{HR}_i$$

where the product is over all selected comorbidities (hypertension, diabetes, smoking, etc.). Independence assumption: This assumes comorbidities multiply independently, which is a simplification — real interactions are often more complex.

Step 5 — User's annual mortality

$$q_{\text{user}}(a, s) = q_{\text{pop}}(a, s) \cdot \mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}}$$

This is the user's estimated annual probability of death at age \(a\).

Step 6 — Plausible range from hazard ratio confidence intervals

The ${citeLink('kokkinos2022')} 95% CI is 0.85–0.87 per MET. Each CI bound uses its own normalization constant so that the population-average HR = 1.0 property is maintained at every CI level:

$$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{lo}} = k_{0.85}(a,s) \cdot 0.85^{\,\mathrm{MET}}, \quad \mathrm{HR}_{\text{hi}} = k_{0.87}(a,s) \cdot 0.87^{\,\mathrm{MET}}$$

where \(k_{0.85}\) and \(k_{0.87}\) are computed analogously to \(k\) but using the respective HR-per-MET value. We then compute \(q_{\text{lo}} = q_{\text{pop}} \times \mathrm{HR}_{\text{lo}} \times \mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}}\) and similarly for hi. The displayed range is labeled "plausible" rather than "95% CI" because this simultaneous variation is conservative.

Step 7 — Life expectancy

For the user's current mortality multiplier \(m = \mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}}\), the remaining life expectancy from age \(a\) is:

$$\mathrm{LE} = \sum_{x=a}^{118} \prod_{t=a}^{x} \bigl(1 - \min(m \cdot q_{\text{pop}}(t,s),\; 1)\bigr)$$

(This is the standard actuarial formula: each term is the probability of surviving to the start of year \(x+1\), and each year of survival contributes 1 to the sum.)

Life expectancy delta. The impact of moving to a different fitness level (e.g., 90th percentile) is:

$$\Delta\mathrm{LE} = \mathrm{LE}_{\text{target}} - \mathrm{LE}_{\text{current}}$$

Simplifying assumptions and limitations: We apply the hazard multiplier uniformly across all future ages, assuming fitness remains constant for life. In reality, fitness may decline with age, and comorbidities interact non-multiplicatively. The LE estimates should be interpreted as illustrative comparisons, not clinical predictions of causal benefit.

Peer Comparison: FRIEND Registry

The ${citeLink('friend2022')} provides VO₂ max percentile norms from a large US cohort of directly measured cardiopulmonary exercise tests. The calculator uses these percentile ranks to estimate where your fitness falls relative to healthy US adults of your age and sex. The percentile is also used in the mortality computation — it determines your position on the continuous fitness hazard curve, which is then scaled by the ${citeLink('kokkinos2022')} hazard ratio.

The risk equivalent calculation converts an annual excess mortality fraction Δq into N equivalent single-event risks: N = Δq / (mortality per event). For example, if moving to a lower fitness category increases your annual mortality by 0.05%, and one base jump carries a 0.043% mortality risk, then 0.05% / 0.043% ≈ 1.2 base jumps per year carries an equivalent annual mortality burden.

Limitations

  1. Causality. Observational hazard ratios from ${citeLink('kokkinos2022')} do not prove causation. Low fitness may partly reflect underlying disease (reverse causation) rather than being a modifiable risk factor. Exercise intervention trials show real but smaller mortality benefits than the observational HRs suggest.
  2. Spline interpolation and extrapolation. The FRIEND 2022 percentile reference values are fitted with custom monotone splines in both directions to ensure smooth monotonicity. The age direction uses a quadratic histogram-preserving spline (bin-average exact on each decade); percentile uses a monotone cubic Hermite spline (PCHIP/Fritsch-Carlson) with physiological extrapolation: p=0 is fixed at ~10 mL/kg/min (clinical floor for ambulatory humans), and p=100 mirrors the 80th→90th gap. Interpolation between published percentiles (10th–90th) is accurate to within ~0.5 mL/kg/min. These extrapolation assumptions are supported by physiological literature and do not rest on empirical data beyond the published FRIEND table.
  3. Risk factor independence. Multiplying hazard ratios assumes each risk factor's effect is independent. Many conditions interact synergistically (e.g., diabetes + hypertension), so combined risk estimates may be biased in either direction.
  4. Hazard ratio consistency across demographics. The ${citeLink('kokkinos2022')} HR of 0.86 per MET is reported as consistent across age, sex, and racial groups with no significant interactions. While this is reassuring, unmeasured effect modifications in other populations or subgroups may exist.
  5. VO₂ max measurement error. Field estimates from wearables or exercise tests carry ±5–15 mL/kg/min uncertainty, large enough to shift percentile rank by ~10 percentiles. This propagates directly into uncertainty in the fitness hazard multiplier.
  6. Relative fitness constancy across the life course. The model applies a fitness hazard multiplier derived from the user's current fitness percentile rank, uniformly to all future ages. The baseline mortality is age-stratified (from SSA life tables). The assumption is that the user's relative fitness rank (percentile) within their age cohort remains stable. In reality, individuals may improve or decline relative to their cohort as they age, or experience major health events that shift their fitness trajectory.
  7. Population generalizability. The ${citeLink('friend2022')} normative data come from a large fitness registry with predominantly White participants. The ${citeLink('kokkinos2022')} hazard ratio (0.86 per MET) was found to be consistent across age, sex, and racial groups, with no significant interactions. However, the underlying cohorts may not fully represent all socioeconomic strata, and results may differ in populations with limited access to fitness testing or in non-US populations.
  8. Comorbidity data limitations. Risk factor HRs are drawn from diverse studies, often non-contemporaneous and using different adjustment methods. Direct comparison assumes consistent methodology, which is not always true.
