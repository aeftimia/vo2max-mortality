Overview

This calculator estimates how a person's annual all-cause mortality probability changes across fitness levels. The same mathematical framework applies to both supported biomarkers (VO₂ max and grip strength). The approach has three steps:

  1. Anchor to population life tables. The give the average annual probability of death \(q(a, s)\) for the US population.
  2. Apply continuous fitness hazard model. Using normative percentile data and published continuous hazard ratios (see table below), we compute a smooth, monotone fitness hazard multiplier normalized so the population average equals 1.0.
  3. Adjust for the user's risk factors. Comorbidity hazard ratios from large published studies are applied multiplicatively to shift the user's personal estimate.

Mathematical Model

Let \(B\) denote the biomarker value (VO₂ max or grip strength), and let \(r(s)\) denote the per-unit hazard ratio (which may depend on sex \(s\) for grip strength, but is sex-invariant for VO₂ max). Let \(d\) be the unit divisor. The model proceeds as follows:

Step 1 — Baseline population mortality

Let \(q_{\text{pop}}(a, s)\) = annual mortality probability from the for age \(a\) and sex \(s\).

Step 2 — Biomarker percentile conversion

For a user with biomarker value \(B\), we estimate their fitness percentile \(p\) (relative rank among adults of the same age and sex) using the inverse of fitted piecewise-cubic (PCHIP) percentile splines. These are constructed in two stages:

  • Age direction — monotone quadratic histospline: Published percentile values are treated as bin averages (not point estimates at midpoints). A C¹ piecewise-quadratic is fitted so that the integral over each age bin exactly reproduces the published value. Monotonicity is enforced.
  • Percentile direction — monotone cubic Hermite (PCHIP): At each integer age, the percentile values from the age histosplines are extended to the 0th and 100th percentile with physiological bounds (see table above for floor values; 100th percentile mirrors the gap between the two highest published percentiles). A C¹ monotone piecewise-cubic spline (Fritsch–Carlson) is fitted through all knots.
  • Both directions guarantee monotonicity, C¹ continuity, and exact polynomial integration. Gauss-Legendre quadrature is used for the exponential HR integral over each polynomial piece.

Step 3 — Continuous fitness hazard ratio

The raw hazard ratio as a function of biomarker value \(B\) is:

$$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{raw}}(B, s) = r(s)^{\,B/d}$$

where \(r(s)\) is the per-unit hazard ratio (protective direction, <1) and \(d\) is the unit divisor. For VO₂ max, \(r = 0.86\) and \(d = 3.5\) (so the exponent is METs). For grip strength, \(r(s) = (1/\mathrm{HR}_{\text{per 5kg lower}}(s))^{1/5}\) and \(d = 1\).

Step 4 — Population-mean normalization

To ensure the population-average fitness hazard equals 1.0 (preserving the baseline life-table mortality), we compute a normalization constant:

$$k(a,s) = \frac{1}{\displaystyle\int_0^1 r(s)^{\,B(p,a,s)/d}\, dp}$$

where \(B(p,a,s)\) is the biomarker value at percentile \(p\) from the fitted splines. The integral is evaluated using 16-point Gauss-Legendre quadrature on each polynomial piece.

Three separate normalization constants are precomputed per (age, sex): one for the central HR estimate and one for each 95% CI bound, ensuring population-average HR = 1.0 at every CI level.

The user's normalized fitness hazard is then:

$$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{fit}}(a, s, B) = k(a,s) \cdot r(s)^{\,B/d}$$

This ensures:

  • The population-weighted average \(\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{HR}_{\text{fit}}] = 1.0\) exactly.
  • Individual biomarker values map smoothly and monotonically to hazard multipliers.
  • Population baseline mortality from SSA life tables is preserved.

Why integrate rather than anchor at the median? Because the exponential function is strictly convex, \(\mathbb{E}[r^{B/d}] \neq r^{\mathbb{E}[B]/d}\). Integrating over the full percentile distribution preserves the actual population mean.

Note on sex-stratified HR (grip strength): Because \(r(s)\) differs between men and women for grip strength, the normalization constant \(k(a,s)\) is computed separately per sex using that sex's HR value. For VO₂ max, \(r\) is sex-invariant (Kokkinos et al. found no significant interactions), so only the percentile distribution differs between sexes.

Step 5 — User risk factor adjustment

Comorbidity hazard ratios from large cohort studies are applied multiplicatively:

$$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}} = \mathrm{HR}_{\text{fit}} \cdot \prod_i \mathrm{HR}_i$$

where the product is over all selected comorbidities. Independence assumption: This assumes comorbidities multiply independently, which is a simplification.

Step 6 — User's annual mortality

$$q_{\text{user}}(a, s) = q_{\text{pop}}(a, s) \cdot \mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}}$$

Step 7 — Plausible range from HR confidence intervals

Each CI bound of the published hazard ratio uses its own normalization constant, so population-average HR = 1.0 is maintained at every CI level:

$$\mathrm{HR}_{\text{lo}} = k_{\text{lo}}(a,s) \cdot r_{\text{lo}}(s)^{\,B/d}, \quad \mathrm{HR}_{\text{hi}} = k_{\text{hi}}(a,s) \cdot r_{\text{hi}}(s)^{\,B/d}$$

The displayed range is labeled "plausible" rather than "95% CI" because this is a simplified uncertainty propagation.

Step 8 — Life expectancy

For the user's mortality multiplier \(m = \mathrm{HR}_{\text{user}}\), remaining life expectancy from age \(a\) is:

$$\mathrm{LE} = \sum_{x=a}^{118} \prod_{t=a}^{x} \bigl(1 - \min(m \cdot q_{\text{pop}}(t,s),\; 1)\bigr)$$

This is the standard actuarial formula. The hazard multiplier is applied uniformly across all future ages, assuming the user's relative fitness rank stays constant. LE estimates should be interpreted as illustrative comparisons, not clinical predictions.

Limitations

  1. Causality. Observational hazard ratios do not prove causation. Low fitness may partly reflect underlying disease (reverse causation) rather than being a purely modifiable risk factor. Exercise intervention trials show real but smaller mortality benefits than the observational HRs suggest.
  2. Spline interpolation and extrapolation. Normative percentile data are fitted with custom monotone splines in both directions. The age direction uses a quadratic histogram-preserving spline (bin-average exact); the percentile direction uses monotone cubic Hermite (PCHIP/Fritsch–Carlson) with physiological extrapolation at the tails. Interpolation between published percentiles is accurate to within ~0.5 units.
  3. Risk factor independence. Multiplying hazard ratios assumes each risk factor's effect is independent. Many conditions interact synergistically (e.g., diabetes + hypertension), so combined risk estimates may be biased in either direction.
  4. Sex-stratified vs. sex-invariant HR. The VO₂ max HR (0.86 per MET) is reported as consistent across age, sex, and racial groups. The grip strength HR differs by sex (1.20 per 5 kg for women vs. 1.16 for men). The calculator handles both cases, but unmeasured effect modifications in other subgroups may exist.
  5. Measurement error. Field estimates of VO₂ max from wearables carry ±5–15 mL/kg/min uncertainty; grip strength varies with dynamometer type and protocol. This propagates into uncertainty in the fitness hazard multiplier.
  6. Relative fitness constancy. The model applies the hazard multiplier from the user's current percentile rank uniformly to all future ages. In reality, individuals may improve or decline relative to their age cohort.
  7. Population generalizability. VO₂ max norms come from a predominantly White US fitness registry; grip norms from an Italian community sample. Results may differ in other populations. The grip strength HR comes from UK Biobank (ages 40–69); extrapolation outside this range carries additional uncertainty.
  8. Comorbidity data limitations. Risk factor HRs are drawn from diverse studies, often non-contemporaneous and using different adjustment methods.
